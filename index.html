Hello! I'm Emily Ding, the first post-baccalaureate student at iSchool in University of Washington. I am a mom who has 1.5 years old little boy, a wife who loves cooking and a volunteer who enjoys teaching coding for kids. I like to know any new technologies and fascinate with design and data virtualization. Also, my interests are UX design/ research and data virtualization. Currently, I finished client-side web development course with HTML, CSS, JS with JQuery, React and Firebase.

Right now, I am pursing a HCI track in Informatics. I spent much of my life in Shanghai, China, which is an international and modernize city. My long-term goal is to bring knowledge in technology field back to my homeland, China.

My Life Moment

Being a mom gave me new perspective how why I need to equip myself. Over and over the years, I was occupied with thinking what I can pass on to my next generation, and I realized that in 21 century, computational thinking programming are best tools to unleash creativity and solve new problems: 3D/virtual reality, drone, autonomous driving will impact all aspects of our society, etc. Since then, obtaining a bachelor degree in science has become my goal. I enrolled in Bellevue College, achieved a 3.95 GPA and transfered to the University of Washington in 2017.

Most of time, I enjoy travelling with my family and friends, and learn the local cultures. Meanwhile, I always think of how to increase more influences in society. I would like to play multiple roles and create more values. Recently, my husband and I piloted a programing camp for kids in Shanghai. Beyond a volunteer, we designed a curriculum that seeing how the kids mastered programming concepts and created their own projects in a few days. It helped me realize that technology could unleash creativity from anyone regardless of their ages and languages. This is the third year we have done. And I am looking forward what we can do advanced in future.

Past Projects

INFO360 Team Project

The Emergency Box, is to provide a beacon that signals for help and facilitates the search and rescue process. This design intervention will allow people who are trapped in their house, after a natural disaster, to attract help from search and rescuers by providing a reliable way to get attention when other forms of communication are not working.
